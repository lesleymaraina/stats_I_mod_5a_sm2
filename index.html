One-Way ANOVA : Definition



One-Way ANOVA

  • statistical method for comparing the means of a continuous outcome across groups defined by one categorical factor
  • evaluates whether the observed differences among sample means are large relative to the variability observed within groups
  • generalizes the two-sample mean comparison to settings with three or more groups

One-Way ANOVA : Definition



One-Way ANOVA

  • one-way refers to the number of explanatory factors in the ANOVA model

one-way ANOVA has:

  • one continuous outcome
  • one categorical factor
  • two or more factor levels

One-Way ANOVA Model

One-way ANOVA model:

\[ y_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_i + \epsilon_{ij} \]

where:

  • \(y_{ij}\) : outcome for observational unit \(j\) in group \(i\)
  • \(\mu\) : overall mean level
  • \(\alpha_i\) : effect of group \(i\)
  • \(\epsilon_{ij}\) : residual variation for observational unit \(j\) in group \(i\)

One-Way ANOVA : Hypotheses


Null hypothesis (H₀): all population group means are equal.

\[ \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu_3 = \cdots = \mu_k \]

Alternative hypothesis (\(H_A\)): at least one population group mean differs from another


Note: alternative hypothesis does not state which groups differ, instead, states that the group means are not all equal

Key Definition : Outcome Variable

Outcome Variable

Outcome variable is the measured quantity being compared across groups


In one-way ANOVA, the outcome is usually continuous

Biomedical examples include:

  • systolic blood pressure
  • PSA level
  • cholesterol level
  • tumor volume
  • gene expression value
  • biomarker concentration

Key Definition : Factor

Factor

A factor is a categorical explanatory variable that assigns each observational unit to a group.


Examples of factors include:

  • treatment group
  • disease subtype
  • tumor stage
  • genotype category
  • hospital unit
  • exposure category

In one-way ANOVA, there is one factor

Key Definition : Levels

Levels

The levels of a factor are the specific groups being compared.


If the factor is treatment group, the levels might be:

  • placebo
  • low-dose drug
  • high-dose drug

One-way ANOVA evaluates whether the population means for these levels appear equal based on the observed data

One-Way ANOVA: Example Scientific Questions

One-way ANOVA can be used when the scientific question asks whether a continuous measurement differs across categories of one grouping variable.

Examples:

  • Do mean PSA levels differ across tumor stage groups?
  • Do mean biomarker concentrations differ across treatment groups?
  • Do mean tumor volumes differ across genotype categories?
  • Do mean blood pressure values differ across lifestyle intervention groups?

Note: each question has one continuous outcome and one categorical factor

Comparison: One-Way ANOVA and t-Test

Feature Independent-samples t-test One-way ANOVA
Outcome Continuous Continuous
Number of groups Two groups Two or more groups
Main test statistic t statistic F statistic
Null hypothesis Two population means are equal All population group means are equal
Primary result Evidence about one mean difference Evidence that at least one group mean differs

Why Not Run Many t-Tests?

Suppose a study compares mean biomarker levels across four treatment groups

Pairwise t-tests would compare:

  • group 1 vs group 2
  • group 1 vs group 3
  • group 1 vs group 4
  • group 2 vs group 3
  • group 2 vs group 4
  • group 3 vs group 4

Possible Outcome:

  • each test creates another opportunity for a false positive result

  • when there are exactly two groups: one-way ANOVA and an independent-samples t-test evaluate the same mean comparison

  • ANOVA extends the mean-comparison logic of the t-test to more than two groups

Variation Within the Model

one-way ANOVA : asks whether group membership explains enough outcome variation to justify rejecting the equal-means null hypothesis

The total variation is separated into:

  • between-group variation: how much the group means differ from the overall mean
  • within-group variation: how much individual observations differ from their own group mean

F statistic : compares these two sources of variation after accounting for their degrees of freedom

Manual Pieces of the ANOVA Calculation

overall_mean <- mean(anova_data$biomarker)

ss_between <- anova_data |>
  group_by(treatment) |>
  summarize(n = n(), group_mean = mean(biomarker), .groups = "drop") |>
  summarize(ss_between = sum(n * (group_mean - overall_mean)^2)) |>
  pull(ss_between)

ss_within <- anova_data |>
  group_by(treatment) |>
  summarize(ss_within = sum((biomarker - mean(biomarker))^2), .groups = "drop") |>
  summarize(ss_within = sum(ss_within)) |>
  pull(ss_within)

c(ss_between = ss_between, ss_within = ss_within)
ss_between  ss_within 
  1721.581   6862.610
  • code calculates two sources of variation
  • ss_between : measures how far group means are from the overall mean
  • ss_within : measures how far individual observations are from their own group mean

Manual F Statistic

k <- nlevels(anova_data$treatment)
n_total <- nrow(anova_data)

df_between <- k - 1
df_within <- n_total - k

ms_between <- ss_between / df_between
ms_within <- ss_within / df_within

f_statistic <- ms_between / ms_within
p_value <- pf(f_statistic, df_between, df_within, lower.tail = FALSE)

c(
  df_between = df_between,
  df_within = df_within,
  ms_between = ms_between,
  ms_within = ms_within,
  F = f_statistic,
  p_value = p_value
)
  df_between    df_within   ms_between    ms_within            F      p_value 
2.000000e+00 1.050000e+02 8.607905e+02 6.535819e+01 1.317035e+01 7.879019e-06
  • F statistic : between-group mean square divided by the within-group mean square

\[ F = \frac{\text{variance between groups}}{\text{variance within groups}} \]

  • larger F statistic : indicates that differences among group means are large relative to the variability observed inside the groups

ANOVA Table

ANOVA table usually includes:

  • Df: degrees of freedom associated with the factor and residual variation
  • Sum Sq: variation attributed to the factor and residual error
  • Mean Sq: sum of squares divided by degrees of freedom
  • F value: ratio of factor mean square to residual mean square
  • Pr(>F): p-value for the overall ANOVA F-test

The treatment p-value evaluates the null hypothesis that all treatment-group population means are equal

  • ANOVA tells you at least one group mean differs

  • model indicates that treatment has a statistically significant effect on biomarker levels:

    • F(2, 105) = 13.17
      • estimated variability due to treatment differences is about 13 times larger than the average unexplained variability within treatment groups
    • p = 7.88 × 10⁻⁶

Note: to identify the specific group differences, perform a post-hoc multi-comparison test [Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD) test]

ANOVA: Check Model Assumptions

  1. normality: observations in group i are assumed to come from a normal distribution
  2. equal (constant) variance: each group is assumed to have the same population variance
  3. independence: observations should be independent of one another

ANOVA Model Assumptions: Residuals by Treatment Group

  • not a formal diagnostic plot

  • Goal: visually assess whether group variances are reasonably similar

  • residual : error or difference between an actual observed data point and the value predicted by a statistical model

  • residuals centered near zero in each group

  • similar box heights (similar variability)

  • no group with substantially larger spread

ANOVA Model Assumptions: Equal Variance [Homoskedacity]

  • residual : error or difference between an actual observed data point and the value predicted by a statistical model

residuals vs Fitted (which = 1) — introduces residual diagnostics

  • residuals centered around zero
  • no obvious curve
  • similar spread across fitted values

ANOVA Model Assumptions: Test for Normality

normal Q-Q Plot (which = 2) — assesses the normality assumption

  • points lying close to the reference line
  • only small deviations at the tails

ANOVA Model Assumptions: Independence

Test: plot maps model errors against the chronological sequence or physical order in which your data was collected

scale-Location Plot (which = 3) — assesses homoscedasticity

Evidence supporting independence:

  • points randomly scattered around zero
  • no upward or downward trend
